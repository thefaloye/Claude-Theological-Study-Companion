# Reference Works — Theological Study Companion

Authoritative lexicons, dictionaries, concordances, and study tools approved for use in the theological-study-companion skill. Load this file when needing detailed guidance on which resource to consult for a specific task.

---

## NT Greek Lexicons

### BDAG — A Greek-English Lexicon of the NT and Other Early Christian Literature
*(Bauer, Danker, Arndt, Gingrich — 3rd ed., 2000)*
**Primary NT Greek lexicon.** Use as the first and default authority for any NT Greek word. Provides full semantic range, grammatical categories, usage examples from NT, LXX, and extrabiblical Greek. Cite as "BDAG" with page number where possible.

### Louw-Nida — Greek-English Lexicon of the NT Based on Semantic Domains
*(Louw & Nida, 1989)*
Use for **semantic domain analysis** — when the goal is to understand the conceptual field a word belongs to, not just its isolated meaning. Especially useful for word studies where distinguishing near-synonyms matters (e.g., *agapaō* vs. *phileō*; *logos* vs. *rhēma*).

### Thayer's — A Greek-English Lexicon of the NT
*(J.H. Thayer, 1889)*
Classical supplement. Use for historical lexical context and patristic usage. Note: predates modern papyri discoveries; use BDAG for primary authority. Still valuable for classical Greek background and older Reformed commentary cross-referencing.

## OT Hebrew & Aramaic Lexicons

### HALOT — The Hebrew and Aramaic Lexicon of the Old Testament
*(Koehler, Baumgartner, Stamm — Eng. trans. 1994–2000)*
**Primary OT Hebrew and Aramaic lexicon.** Most comprehensive modern Hebrew lexicon. Covers etymology, cognate languages, full semantic range. Use as first authority for OT Hebrew word studies. Includes biblical Aramaic.

### BDB — A Hebrew and English Lexicon of the OT
*(Brown, Driver, Briggs, 1906)*
Classical Hebrew lexicon. Use for historical perspective, root analysis, and cross-referencing older Reformed commentaries. Organises under verbal roots — useful for understanding the morphological family of a word. Supplement with HALOT for modern lexicography.

## Theological Wordbooks

### TDNT — Theological Dictionary of the NT
*(Kittel & Friedrich, eds., 10 vols., Eng. trans. 1964–1976)*
Monumental reference for theological word studies in the NT. Traces terms through classical Greek, LXX, Second Temple Judaism, and NT. Note: use with critical awareness — some articles reflect older history-of-religions methodology. Strongest for tracing background and semantic development.

### TWOT — Theological Wordbook of the OT
*(Harris, Archer, Waltke, 1980)*
Standard evangelical reference for OT theological word studies. Organised by root. Provides theological significance, OT usage, and cross-reference to NT. More accessible and confessionally conservative than TDNT's OT equivalents.

### NIDNTT — New International Dictionary of NT Theology
*(Colin Brown, ed., 4 vols., 1975–1985)*
Reformed-friendly alternative to TDNT. More accessible, with broader application. Includes theological and pastoral synthesis sections. Useful for preaching and teaching contexts.

### NIDOTTE — New International Dictionary of OT Theology and Exegesis
*(Willem VanGemeren, ed., 5 vols., 1997)*
Comprehensive evangelical OT theological dictionary. Covers Hebrew/Aramaic terms with theological depth. Reformed-friendly. Excellent for integrating OT word studies with systematic and biblical theology.

## Bible Dictionaries & Encyclopaedias

### ISBE — International Standard Bible Encyclopaedia
*(James Orr, ed., orig. 1915; revised ed. Bromiley, 1979–1988)*
Standard evangelical Bible encyclopaedia. Covers biblical history, geography, archaeology, persons, and theology. Revised edition updated with modern scholarship while retaining evangelical commitments. Use for background, cultural context, and introductory theological summaries.

### Vine's Expository Dictionary of OT and NT Words
*(W.E. Vine, M.F. Unger, W. White Jr.)*
Accessible theological word studies keyed to Strong's numbers. Good entry point for word studies before going to BDAG/HALOT. Note: not a technical lexicon; use as a study aid, not a primary linguistic authority. Evangelically conservative.

### Zondervan Pictorial Bible Dictionary
*(Merrill C. Tenney, ed.)*
One-volume encyclopaedia for quick reference on persons, places, customs, and doctrines. Use for rapid contextual orientation. Conservative evangelical.

## Concordances

### Strong's Exhaustive Concordance
*(James Strong, 1890)*
Indexes every word in the KJV with original language numbers. Use for locating occurrences, identifying the Hebrew/Greek term behind an English word, and broad word-occurrence surveys. Note: Strong's numbers are a reference tool, not a substitute for lexical work in BDAG/HALOT.

### Young's Analytical Concordance
*(Robert Young, 1879)*
Groups English words by the original language term they translate. Especially useful for identifying *which* Greek or Hebrew word underlies a given English rendering — helpful when translations differ and you want to isolate the original term in question.

### Nave's Topical Bible
*(Orville J. Nave, 1896)*
Topical arrangement of Scripture references by subject. Use for thematic cross-referencing and locating relevant passages for a doctrinal study or sermon. Does not replace a theological index but is a useful survey tool.

## Usage Notes

- Always prefer **primary lexicons** (BDAG, HALOT, BDB) over secondary summaries (Vine's, Strong's numbers) for exegetical conclusions
- Use Louw-Nida for **semantic domain** comparison, not as a standalone word study tool
- TDNT and TWOT are for **theological wordbook** depth — after establishing the basic lexical meaning, not before
- Concordances are **location tools** — use them to find texts, then do the exegetical work in the text itself
- All reference works should be used to serve the **grammatical-historical reading of Scripture**, not to substitute for it
